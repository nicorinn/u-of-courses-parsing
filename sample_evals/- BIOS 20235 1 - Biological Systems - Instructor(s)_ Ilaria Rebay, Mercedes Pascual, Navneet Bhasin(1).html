What was the most important thing (to you) that you learned in this course? What aspect of the material is still unclear for you, that you wish you could have learned better?

Table for [QTitle].
Comments
The most important thing I learned in this course was the genetic toolkit for designing experiments. I wish the second part of the course was a little longer, but that's due to covid scheduling.
The most important thing that I gained from this course was the furthering of my understanding of research papers and the overall scientific process. I specifically gained understanding of how genetic experiments can be used to look at different biological processes. One specific aspect of this course that I really did not understand was the embryology aspect and some of the ecology aspects.
I think that genetic screens were probably the thing which will stick with me most. I think I was slightly vaguer on the evolution, but that makes sense given how little time we had.
Basis of molecular genetics and how to think like a geneticist
I really appreciate the opportunity to learn how to read research papers, which I was lucky to have training in the last two quarters through various topics. The paper reading skills I know will benefit me a long way.

I wish we had been able to spend a little more time on Professor Pascual's portion of the class, because I think knowledge related to ecology and evolution was valuable and important, and I wish we weren't in such a rush to finish the course.
The concept of genetic screens is the most important. Professor Rebay did an excellent job teaching us how those work and then showing us lots of examples of how they can be used to study lots of things.
I learned how to think about scientific questions that may not have a clear answer. Part of this was thinking about factors that might contribute to a given phenotype or experimental result as well as what unexpected factors could cause us to misinterpret the result.
Some of the crosses are still a bit unclear to me. I understand all the examples we did in class but still had a hard time working through crosses on my own. I think some more examples/practice problems (in class with feedback, not necessarily psets) would've been helpful for this.
The most important thing I learned was basic genetic experimental approaches. This gave me a basic understanding of how to understand many genetic experiments and to make predictions about genetic mechanisms
Lab skills were an important thing I learned in this course, and generally how to approach problems like scientists (thinking about the scientific method).
I wish we had had more time learning ecology and evolution, although I don't wish we had lost any time on genetics and developmental biology.
The second half with the instructor shift is quite unclear to me.

What observational, analytical, or technical skills did you gain during the laboratory exercises that enhanced your understanding of how biologists answer questions in this particular field?

Table for [QTitle].
Comments
I gained many technical skills, most notably handling and phenotyping Drosophila.
We learned how to conduct fly crosses and sex and determine other unique phenotypes of flies and examine yeast. We also learned how to view embryos under a microscope. Overall, these were pretty specialized topics but it really helped us apply the concepts we were learning in the first 2/3rds of the quarter with Professor Rebay.
We performed genetic screens, complementation tests, and mapped mutations. All of these are very interesting.
How to work with genetic crosses, writing them out, scoring phenotypes
Doing genetic screens with flies was very helpful to apply the theoretical concepts learned in lecture to lab! I also understood the amount of work and type of work (at least a portion of it that I was introduced to in this class) that geneticists needed to do.
I really enjoyed all our experiments with flies. Learning how to identify various phenotypes and do crosses was very useful.
I learned about transposons, genetic mapping, complementation testing, etc.
How to perform/design crosses. Work with drosophila and yeast. how to perform mutagenesis.
Learning to cross and screen flies gave me a greater appreciation for the amount of time it takes to conduct drosophila experiments. It also helped enhance my understanding of segregation analysis. Furthermore, doing IPCR and CRISPR mutagenesis was very interesting!
We learned how to carry out a lab from start to finish, including the collection and analysis of the data.
